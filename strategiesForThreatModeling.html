Threat Modeling Strategies

Finding security problems before they happen. Discovering bugs during design makes fixing them exponentially easier than waiting for the final build.

The Approaches to Building

Methods vary from rigid "model kits" meant for one specific outcome, to versatile "building blocks" that can construct anything. Finding the right fit is crucial.

Ways to Start

1. Simple Questions & Brainstorming

Asking "What is your threat model?" works rapidly for security experts, but leaves beginners without a structural net.

2. Structured Brainstorming

Using techniques like "movie plotting" or "pre-mortems" (imagining failure). Better, but still not universally effective.

Structure vs. Required Expertise

Higher structure requires less innate expertise.

The Three Main Strategies

Evaluating the focal points of your security analysis. While it is tempting to focus on assets or attackers, analyzing the software itself yields the most actionable results.

💎

Asset-Centered

Focuses on valuable data, money, or hardware.

The Flaw "Asset" has confusing definitions (targets vs. stepping stones). It fails to explain how things will break.
🦹

Attacker-Centered

Focuses on the psychology and goals of the hacker.

The Flaw Humans are unpredictable. It leads to unresolvable arguments ("no one would do that") and unfixable human-centric threats.
💻

Software-Centered

Focuses strictly on the code and system architecture.

The Verdict The most effective strategy for the vast majority of teams, providing actionable, fixable engineering tasks.

Key Terms Glossary

Threat Modeling

The process of looking at a system to find possible security holes or "threats" before they manifest.

Assets

The valuable parts of your system, like customer credit card numbers, intellectual property, or the servers themselves.

Stepping Stones

Less important parts of a system that a hacker breaks into first, used as leverage to reach the high-value assets.

Gate Releases

A strict rule ensuring "We cannot launch this software until it passes this specific security tracking check."

Scope

The boundaries of what a project covers. Focusing strictly on what needs to be modeled versus operational processes.

Tracking Item

A tool (digital to-do list, bug report, checklist) used to guarantee diagrams and threats are re-checked.
