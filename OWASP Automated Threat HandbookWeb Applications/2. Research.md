The creators of this handbook didn't just guess which threats were important. They spent months digging through over 150 sources—like academic papers, hacker blog posts, and security reports. They found 600 different "data points" (bits of info about attacks).

Then, they played a massive game of "keep, toss, or combine." They threw out anything that was just a coding mistake (like a typo in the code) and kept the "Abuse of Functionality" issues. They eventually narrowed it down from 600 points to 19 official names. They also checked their work against other famous security lists (like the OWASP Top 10) to make sure they weren't just repeating what everyone else already knew.

## 🔑 Key Concepts

- **Literature Review:** A deep dive into existing research to see what is already known about a topic. 📚
    
- **Data Points:** Individual pieces of information or examples of attacks found during research. 📍
    
- **De-duplication:** The process of removing repeat information so you only have unique items left. ✂️
    
- **Ontology:** (Reminder) The formal naming and definition of the threats. 🏷️
    
- **CAPEC / WASC / CWE:** These are "dictionaries" of security problems. Think of them as the "encyclopedias" of how hackers break things. 📖
    

## ⚠️ Threat Explanation

In a real web app, the "threat" described here is **Inconsistency**. Before this book, one company might call a bot attack "Credential Stuffing," while another called it "Brute Force," and another called it "Account Takeover." This research fixed that confusion so bug hunters and companies can speak the same language. 🗣️

## 🛠 Methodology

The researchers followed this specific workflow:

1. **Gather:** Collected 150+ sources and 600 data points.
    
2. **Filter:** Excluded "Implementation Bugs" (coding errors).
    
3. **Map:** Created a large diagram to see how different attacks relate to each other.
    
4. **Distill:** Reduced 40 "clusters" of threats down to 20, and finally down to **19 unique threat events**.
    
5. **Peer Review:** They asked 60,000+ people and experts at conferences to double-check their list.
    
6. **Cross-Reference:** Compared the list to CAPEC and WASC to find gaps.

## 🏗 Example Scenario

Imagine you find a way to make a web server slow by requesting a very "heavy" search results page 1,000 times.

- **Old Way:** You report it as "Application DoS."
    
- **The Handbook Way:** Because of this research, you can look closer. Is it actually **OAT-015 (Denial of Service)** or is it a side effect of **OAT-005 (Scraping)**? Identifying the _intent_ (the "What is happening right now?") is what this research allows you to do.
    

## 💡 Important Notes

- **The "Gap":** Notice that the **OWASP Top 10** doesn't really cover these automated abuses! This is why hackers who study this handbook find bugs that others miss. 🕵️
    
- **Abuse of Functionality:** This is the "magic phrase." Almost all of these 19 threats fall under this one category in other dictionaries (like CAPEC-210).
    
- **Not for "Bugs":** If you find a classic Buffer Overflow, this handbook isn't the right tool. This is for **logic and automation**.
