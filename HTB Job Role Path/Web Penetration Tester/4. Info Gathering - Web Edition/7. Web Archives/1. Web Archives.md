### What is the Wayback Machine?
![[wayback.png]]
The internet moves very fast. Websites change or disappear every day. The **Wayback Machine** is like a huge digital library for the whole internet. It is run by a group called the **Internet Archive**, which is a non-profit (a company that works to help people rather than make money). They have been saving copies of websites since 1996.

This tool lets you "travel back in time." You can see exactly how a website looked years ago. These saved copies are called **snapshots** or **archives**. They show you the old design, the old writing, and how the site used to work.

### How Does It Work?

The Wayback Machine uses **Web Crawlers** (also called **Bots**). These are automatic programs that act like robots. They browse the internet by clicking every link they find, just like a human would.

While a search engine like Google only "reads" a page to list it in search results, the Wayback Machine **downloads** everything. This includes:

- **HTML:** The basic structure and text of the page.
    
- **CSS:** The code that makes the site look pretty with colors and fonts.
    
- **JavaScript:** The code that makes buttons and interactive parts work.
    
- **Images:** All the pictures and icons.
    

#### The Three-Step Process:
![[ig_webarchives_1.png]]
1. **Crawling:** The bots wander from link to link. When they find a page, they make a copy of it.
    
2. **Archiving:** The copies are saved in a giant storage system. Each copy is tagged with the exact date and time it was found. Popular sites get copied every day, while quiet sites might only get copied once a year.
    
3. **Accessing:** You go to the Wayback Machine website, type in a web address (URL), and pick a date on a calendar to see that version.
    

### Important Things to Know

- **Not Everything is Saved:** The system focuses on sites that are important for history or research. It doesn't save every single page on the internet.
    
- **Privacy:** If a website owner asks the Internet Archive to stop saving their site, they usually listen, though it isn't always a 100% guarantee.
    

### Why is this useful for "Web Reconnaissance"?

**Web Reconnaissance** (which means "scouting" or gathering information about a target) is easier with the Wayback Machine. It helps you find things the owners might have tried to hide or delete:

- **Finding Hidden Secrets:** You might find old pages or files that are deleted now but still have sensitive info or security holes (**vulnerabilities**).
    
- **Watching Changes:** By looking at old versions, you can see how a company changed its technology or its staff over time.
    
- **Gathering Intelligence:** You can find old names of employees or old marketing plans. This is called **OSINT** (Open Source Intelligence), which means finding info that is legally available to the public.
    
- **Staying Stealthy:** Looking at the Wayback Machine is "passive." You aren't touching the actual target's website, so they don't know you are looking at them.
    

### Example: HackTheBox (HTB)

If you wanted to see the very first version of the "HackTheBox" website, you would put their address into the Wayback Machine. You would find that the earliest save happened on **June 10, 2017, at 4:23 AM**.

**Key Terms Defined:**

- **URL:** A web address (like www.google.com).
    
- **Snapshot/Capture:** A saved "picture" of a website at a specific moment.
    
- **Vulnerability:** A weakness or "hole" in a computer system that could let a hacker in.
    
- **Infrastructure:** The underlying hardware and software that keeps a website running.
