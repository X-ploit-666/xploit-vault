## 1. The Big Picture (Plain English Summary)

**Crawling** (also known as **spidering**) is an automated way of exploring the internet. Imagine a robot that acts like a spider. Instead of a physical web, it walks through the "World Wide Web" by clicking on every link it finds.

A **web crawler** is a computer program (a **bot**) that follows a set of rules called **algorithms**. These rules tell the bot how to find and record information so that search engines (like Google) can show you results, or so researchers can analyze data.

### How it works:

It all starts with a **seed URL**. A URL is just a website address, and the "seed" is the very first page the crawler visits.

1. The crawler visits the seed page.
    
2. It reads everything on that page (**parsing**).
    
3. It finds all the links to other pages (**extracting**).
    
4. it puts those new links into a "to-do list" (**queue**).
    
5. It visits the next link and repeats the cycle over and over.
    

This is different from "fuzzing." Fuzzing is guessing what a link might be. Crawling is actually following real, existing paths.

## 2. Code Breakdown

The original text used a tree-style text diagram to show how a crawl moves from one page to another.

### Example 1: Starting at the Homepage

```
Homepage
├── link1
├── link2
└── link3
```

- **What this does:** This shows the "Seed URL" (the Homepage).
    
- **How it works:** The crawler scans the Homepage and finds three paths it can take: `link1`, `link2`, and `link3`. It hasn't visited them yet; it just discovered they exist.
    

### Example 2: Moving to the Next Level

```
link1 Page
├── Homepage
├── link2
├── link4
└── link5
```

- **What this does:** The crawler has now moved from the Homepage to the `link1` page.
    
- **How it works:** On the `link1` page, it finds links back to the `Homepage` and `link2`, but it also discovers brand new pages: `link4` and `link5`.
    
- **Importance:** This shows how the crawler’s "to-do list" (the queue) grows as it explores deeper.
    

## 3. Two Ways to Crawl (Strategies)

### A. Breadth-First Crawling (The "Wide" Approach)
![[ig_crawling_1.png]]
- **The Idea:** Explore the entire surface before going deep.
    
- **How it looks:** If a page has 10 links, the crawler visits all 10 links first. Only after those 10 are done does it look at the links _inside_ those pages.
    
- **Why use it:** It's great for getting a quick map of the whole website's structure.
    

### B. Depth-First Crawling (The "Deep" Approach)
![[depth-crawling.svg]]
- **The Idea:** Pick one path and follow it to the very end.
    
- **How it looks:** The crawler clicks a link, then clicks a link on that new page, then another, going as deep as possible. If it hits a dead end, it goes back (**backtracking**) and tries a different path.
    
- **Why use it:** It's useful if you are looking for one specific thing hidden deep inside a site.
    

## 4. What Information is Stolen or Collected?

Crawlers look for specific "prizes" during their journey:

1. **Links:** They collect **Internal Links** (links to pages on the same site) and **External Links** (links to different websites). This helps map how everything is connected.
    
2. **Comments:** They read sections where people talk. People often accidentally post private details or "vulnerabilities" (weak spots in security) in blog comments or forums.
    
3. **Metadata:** This is **"data about data."** It's hidden info like the page's title, the author's name, or secret keywords. It helps the crawler understand the page's purpose.
    
4. **Sensitive Files:** These are files that should be private but were left open by mistake.
    
    - **Backup Files (`.bak`, `.old`):** Copies of old site data.
        
    - **Configuration Files (`web.config`):** Settings that might contain passwords or **API Keys** (special codes that let programs talk to each other).
        
    - **Log Files:** Records of errors or who visited the site.
        

## 5. Connecting the Dots (The Importance of Context)

One single piece of info usually isn't enough to cause trouble. The real power comes from **Context**—looking at how different pieces of info fit together.

**Example of "Connecting the Dots":**

- **Point A:** A crawler finds a list of links that all go to a folder called `/files/`.
    
- **Point B:** You visit that folder and realize "directory browsing" is on (meaning anyone can see a list of every file in that folder).
    
- **Point C:** You find a comment on a blog where a user mentions a "file server."
    
- **The Result:** By combining these, you realize the `/files/` folder is likely the "file server" mentioned, and it's accidentally exposing secret company documents.
