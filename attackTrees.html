Mapping the Threat Landscape

Attack trees provide a formal, methodical way to organize security threats. By modeling a system with the attacker's ultimate goal at the root, we systematically break down the paths they could take to compromise our defenses.

Core Objective

Find new vulnerabilities, organize known threats, and prioritize defenses by evaluating the exact paths an attacker must take to execute an exploit.

◉ Method Selection

There are three primary approaches to utilizing attack trees in threat modeling. Pre-existing models offer quick insights, while custom-built models provide deep project-specific accuracy.

▲ Logical Architecture

Trees operate on boolean logic. "OR" nodes indicate an attacker only needs one path to succeed. "AND" nodes mean an attacker must complete all listed steps to proceed.

The 6-Step Construction Process

A systematic approach to building highly effective, actionable attack trees without getting lost in infinite possibilities.

01

Representation

Choose the visual style before starting. Will this be a graphical diagram or a text-based outline? Decide early.

02

Root Node

Define the primary target. What is the ultimate goal of the attacker? E.g., "Steal Customer Data" or "Access Server."

03

Subnodes

Break the goal down. Use brainstorming or STRIDE methodology to find the immediate steps required to reach the parent goal.

04

Completeness

Review the branches. Did you miss any major attack vectors? Check standard security frameworks to ensure full coverage.

05

Pruning

Eliminate the noise. Mark attacks that are physically impossible or already thoroughly mitigated so teams don't waste time on them.

06

Presentation

Format for human readability. Consolidate logic, use clear grids, and aim to keep the entire structure viewable on a single page.
