# Threat Modeling: A Simple Guide

### What is Threat Modeling?

Threat modeling is a way for anyone to find security problems in a system. You do this by looking at the "big picture" of how something works instead of looking at every tiny line of computer code.

Think of it like looking at a map of a house to find where a thief might get in, rather than looking at the type of wood used in the front door. You do this so you can find problems before you even finish building the software.

### The Four Steps of Threat Modeling

To do this well, you follow four basic questions:

1. **What are we building?** You start by drawing a simple diagram (a picture) of your software on a whiteboard or paper.
    
2. **What can go wrong?** You look at that picture and try to imagine every way someone could attack it.
    
3. **What are we going to do about it?** You decide how to fix or block those attacks.
    
4. **Did we do a good job?** You check your work to make sure you didn't miss anything.
    

### Understanding the STRIDE Model

**STRIDE** is a checklist used to help you remember what kinds of "bad things" can happen. Here is what each letter means in simple words:

- **S - Spoofing:** Someone pretending to be someone else (like using a fake ID).
    
- **T - Tampering:** Someone changing data or files they aren't supposed to touch (like changing the price of an item in a store database).
    
- **R - Repudiation:** Someone doing something bad and then claiming they didn't do it (like a user saying "I never sent that email").
    
- **I - Information Disclosure:** Private secrets getting out to people who shouldn't see them (like a data leak).
    
- **D - Denial of Service:** Breaking a system so that real users can't use it (like crashing a website so no one can log in).
    
- **E - Elevation of Privilege:** Someone getting "Super User" powers they shouldn't have (like a regular employee getting the Boss's password).

### How to Check Your Work

Once you are done, you should be able to answer "Yes" to these questions:

1. Did we write down every threat we found?
    
2. Do we have a plan to fix every threat?
    
3. Did we create a test to see if the fix works?
    
4. Did the software pass that test?
    

### Summary

Anyone can learn to do this. You just need a drawing of your system and a list of threats (like STRIDE). It helps developers build better things, helps administrators set them up safely, and helps security experts teach others how to stay protected.
