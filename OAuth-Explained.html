🗝️ OAuth Explained

Stop Sharing Your
Master Password

Imagine using a new app that wants your contacts. Giving them your password gives them full control. OAuth is the technology that safely says: "You can access only this specific part, and nothing else."

The Core Problem: Over-Sharing

Historically, to let a new app access your data on another platform, you had to hand over your login credentials. This exposes your entire digital life to third parties. OAuth fixes this by replacing passwords with limited-access tokens.

⚠️

Sharing your password gives an app 100% access to read, write, and delete your data. OAuth restricts this to precisely what is needed.

How It Works (The Simple Steps)

A secure, transparent conversation between the App, You, and the Data Owner.

📱

1. The Request

A new app asks, “Can I access some of your specific data to work properly?”

🔄

2. The Redirect

You are sent securely to the website that actually owns your data (like Google).

🔒

3. The Safe Login

You log in directly on the owning site. Your password stays completely safe with them.

✋

4. The Permission

That website asks you: “Do you explicitly allow this app to access [Specific Data]?”

🎫

5. The Secret Key

If you agree, the app is issued a special, temporary key known as a Token.

🎯

6. Limited Access

The app uses that key to access only what you allowed—and absolutely nothing more.

The Hotel Analogy

Think of your digital account like a hotel. Your password is the Master Key—it opens every single door in the building. You would never give this to a stranger like a delivery person.

  • ✅ Limited Access: OAuth is a Key Card. It only opens the necessary doors (like the gym).
  • ✅ Temporary: The front desk can set the Key Card to expire automatically.
  • ✅ Secure: It never reveals the shape or cut of your original Master Key.

Real-World: "Login with Google"

When you click "Login with Google" on a website, the site sends you to Google's official page. You log in there, meaning the new site never sees your typing.

Google simply asks if the site can see your name and email. Once accepted, the site gets a token. It uses this token to create your account securely.

"At no point does the site ever see your Google password."

The Key Idea

OAuth is a way to give limited, controlled access to your data without sharing your password.
